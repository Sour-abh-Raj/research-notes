# Understanding Literature Reviews: Why They Matter and What Makes One Good

Most of us are taught to *read* literature long before anyone teaches us to *review* it.
We learn to write the introduction of an empirical paper — a few selective citations marshalled
into an argument for why our own study deserves to exist — and we quietly assume a literature
review is just a longer version of that. It isn't. A literature review is a distinct research
design in its own right, with its own logic, its own methodology, and its own standards of rigour.

<!-- more -->

This first post in the *Literature Reviews* series is a reader's introduction to that idea. It
leans on a paper I think every research student should read early:

> Siddaway, A. P., Wood, A. M., & Hedges, L. V. (2019). *How to Do a Systematic Review: A Best
> Practice Guide for Conducting and Reporting Narrative Reviews, Meta-Analyses, and Meta-Syntheses.*
> **Annual Review of Psychology, 70**, 747–770.
> [doi:10.1146/annurev-psych-010418-102803](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-psych-010418-102803)

The paper is written for psychologists, but the authors deliberately pitch it at a high level of
abstraction, and almost everything in it transfers to any field with a literature large enough to
get lost in — which, today, is essentially every field.

## Why a review is one of the best things a student can write

Siddaway and colleagues open with a simple observation. In the early decades of a science, the
literature on any topic was small enough that one person could read and hold all of it. That world
is gone. Literatures now expand exponentially, findings conflict, and it is genuinely unclear which
results are reliable, which replicate, and which questions remain open. **A literature review is the
proposed solution to that complexity** — a way of bringing a body of evidence together,
synthesizing it, and producing an honest picture of what we know and what we don't.

For a master's or PhD student specifically, this makes a review unusually good value for effort, for
two reasons worth being explicit about:

- **Reviews are relatively likely to get published and cited.** High-quality reviews sit near the
  top of the evidence hierarchy and tend to be influential — they are, by their nature, more
  comprehensive and less biased than any single study, which makes editors and reviewers receptive
  to them. A well-executed review is a genuine, substantive contribution to knowledge, not a warm-up
  exercise.
- **Reviews serve the community's time.** When you synthesize a scattered literature, you save every
  future reader the weeks of work you just did — and, more importantly, you surface *research gaps*:
  questions where the evidence is thin, absent, or poor in quality. Those gaps are often invisible
  from inside any single study and only become visible when someone zooms out. Pointing to them is
  one of the most useful things a review can do, and a natural launch-pad for a student's own
  subsequent work.

The authors also note a less glamorous but real motivation: a review is often simply *required* —
to demonstrate command of a topic, or to justify a grant proposal. The trap, especially for novices,
is to treat that requirement as a license to "summarize everything I came across." That produces a
collection of unconnected findings with no critical thought in it. The whole point of what follows
is to sidestep that trap.

## What separates a good review from a summary

This is the question I care about most, and the paper is refreshingly concrete about it. A review
earns its keep when it does at least one of the following:

1. **Develops a new theory, or evaluates an existing one** — using the assembled evidence to explain
   *how and why* the individual studies fit together, rather than just listing what each one found.
2. **Has clear implications for policy or practice** — it changes what a practitioner or
   decision-maker should actually do.
3. **Reveals inadequacies in the literature** — contradictions, gaps, and inconsistencies, *and* an
   exploration of the reasons behind them.

Notice the through-line: in every case the value comes from **integration**, not enumeration. As the
authors put it (borrowing from Baumeister & Leary), the reviewer's job is to ask of each study what
makes it distinct and how it relates to the integrative themes — to make the connections "clear and
explicit." The whole is supposed to be greater than the sum of its parts. A summary leaves the parts
lying side by side; a review fuses them into a claim about the literature as a whole.

## The two non-negotiables: methodical and reproducible

If integration is the *goal*, method is how you earn the right to be believed. The defining feature
of a systematic review is that it is **methodical, transparent, and replicable**: a comprehensive
search for relevant work, explicit and consistently applied rules, and a presentation that another
researcher could in principle follow to the same conclusions.

A point I found liberating: **you are allowed to devise your own methodology.** There is no single
sanctioned recipe. What is *not* optional is that your method makes sense and is stated openly. The
authors recommend adopting the mindset of a **judge and jury** rather than a lawyer — you are trying
to reach the fairest verdict the evidence allows, not to win an argument you have already chosen.
(The introduction of an empirical paper is written like a lawyer; a review must not be.)

## The central cognitive task: deciding what to keep

Here is the heart of it, and the part the paper kept returning to. The single most important — and
most genuinely *cognitive* — act of reviewing is deciding **what to include and what to exclude.**
Your inclusion and exclusion criteria define the boundaries of the entire review, and readers will
interpret every conclusion through them.

Best practice is to set those criteria *before* you encounter the literature, based purely on your
research questions, so they aren't quietly bent to fit whatever studies happen to exist — and then to
apply them *consistently*. Common criteria concern the research question and scope, how a concept is
defined, what is measured and how, research design, the population studied, and the time frame.

But the honest difficulty is this: **you often don't yet know enough to set good criteria.** If you
were already an expert on the topic you probably wouldn't need to review it. So the reviewer is, in a
real sense, making the best judgement available given incomplete knowledge — and the paper is candid
that this is the norm, not a failure. The recommended response is not to pretend otherwise but to:

- **revisit and reflect** on the criteria as your understanding grows (some cycling is expected),
- handle **borderline cases** deliberately — with explicit discussion, recourse to theory, and ideally
  a second reader to share the decision — and revise the criteria if a borderline case exposes a real
  flaw, then re-run the search,
- and **keep meticulous records** of every decision and its reason, because over months and thousands
  of studies you will not remember why you excluded something.

What is *never* acceptable is applying criteria inconsistently or hiding how the sifting was done.
The flexibility is in choosing the rules; the discipline is in applying and reporting them.

## When you have no idea where to start: read other reviews

If you genuinely don't know how to build a methodology, or how to frame your inclusion and exclusion
criteria, the paper gives a concrete first move that I'd underline for any beginner: **go and read
existing literature reviews in your area** — and, ideally, a few high-quality systematic reviews from
top journals in your field — and study *how they made these decisions.*

This does double duty. Searching for prior reviews tells you whether the review you're planning has
already been done (saving you wasted months, or giving you a clean rationale for an *update*). And
reading them turns the abstract best-practice principles into something concrete: you see what real
search strategies, real criteria, and real synthesis actually look like on the page. Once you have
that footing, you can set your own parameters — and, where possible, discuss them with a collaborator
or supervisor, who acts as a second integrator on the hardest calls.

## Absence of evidence is a finding, not an embarrassment

A review should **show its evidence explicitly** and let readers appraise the quality of what it
rests on. One consequence of this is easy to miss and genuinely reassuring:

> If the evidence is thin, conflicting, or simply absent, **that is itself a finding.** Reporting it
> does not weaken the review — it strengthens it.

The paper makes the same point about counterexamples, quoting Baumeister & Leary: if there are
important exceptions to your general pattern, acknowledging them *strengthens* the review, and the
argument can often be built further by recognising the moderators and boundary conditions they
reveal. And if the apparent exceptions don't, on close inspection, contradict the main pattern — then
pointing that out strengthens the manuscript too. A review that quietly buries inconvenient evidence
is doing the opposite of its job. One that names it, and shows it doesn't overturn the overall
picture, is being exactly as honest as a judge and jury should be.

This also licenses a perfectly legitimate conclusion that anxious students often feel they're not
allowed to reach: that **the current evidence is inadequate to answer the question**, or even that it
challenges a favoured theory. If that's what the evidence says, saying so is a contribution.

## Don't summarize — operationalize

The line I'll keep coming back to: **a good review does not merely summarize findings; it
operationalizes concepts.** Rather than restating that "Study A found X," the work is to *zoom out* —
to group and link concepts, critique them, draw attention to conceptual and methodological issues,
and assess the strength of the overall evidence for each main point.

Operationalizing a concept means making it precise enough to show its **incremental value** — what it
adds beyond what we already had — and its **applicability**: whether and how it actually holds for a
particular problem, in a particular context. A useful presentation habit the authors borrow for this
is to state findings in context — roughly, "in a sample of A, method B produced result C, supporting
the view that X causes Y" — so the reader can always judge the *quality and nature* of the evidence
behind each claim, not just the claim itself.

## A permission you didn't know you had: grey literature

Here's something the paper makes explicit that surprises a lot of students: **you are allowed — in
fact, encouraged — to use grey literature.** Because a systematic review aims to be *comprehensive*
and to push back against **publication bias** (the tendency for only statistically significant
results to ever get published), restricting yourself to peer-reviewed journals can quietly bias the
whole picture. Grey literature is anything produced outside commercial publishing: technical and
government reports, working papers, dissertations and theses, conference proceedings, and official
publications. Including credible unpublished work that meets your inclusion criteria makes a review
*stronger*, not less legitimate — provided you hold it to the same criteria and record-keeping as
everything else.

Sources the paper points to:

- **[OpenGrey](http://www.opengrey.eu)** — a European grey-literature database
- **[OpenDOAR](https://v2.sherpa.ac.uk/opendoar/)** — Directory of Open Access Repositories; searches
  the open-access repositories of thousands of universities worldwide
- **WorldCat** — for dissertations and theses
- **Google and Google Scholar** — surprisingly effective for dissertations and work by societies and
  charities

Beyond those, here are the **free, easily usable sources I lean on** for triangulating a literature —
all open to anyone, and several with clean APIs if you want to search programmatically:

- **[arXiv](https://arxiv.org)** — the preprint server where much of the work in CS, machine learning,
  and statistics appears *first*, often months before formal publication
- **[OpenAlex](https://openalex.org)** — a free, open index of hundreds of millions of scholarly
  works, authors, and citations (the open successor to Microsoft Academic Graph), excellent for
  comprehensive, programmatic searching
- **[AIS eLibrary (AISeL)](https://aisel.aisnet.org)** — the canonical repository for Information
  Systems research: ICIS, ECIS, and the AIS conference and journal ecosystem
- **[SSRN](https://www.ssrn.com)** — working papers and preprints across management, IS, economics,
  and the social sciences, where a lot of work circulates well before formal publication
- **[CORE](https://core.ac.uk)** — aggregates full-text open-access papers from thousands of
  repositories worldwide; ideal for comprehensive sweeps
- **[Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org)** — AI-assisted search with a strong free API
  for reproducible, programmatic searching
- **[BASE](https://www.base-search.net)** — the Bielefeld Academic Search Engine, one of the largest
  open-access academic search engines

Triangulating across several of these — rather than trusting any single database — is one of the
simplest ways to make a search genuinely comprehensive.

## Takeaways

If I compress the paper into things to carry forward:

- A literature review is a **research design**, not a long introduction — and for a student it's
  high-leverage: publishable, citable, and a natural way to find your own research gap.
- A good review **integrates**: it develops or tests theory, informs practice, or exposes the
  literature's inadequacies — it never just enumerates.
- Be **methodical and reproducible**. You may invent your method; you may not hide it. Think judge and
  jury, not lawyer.
- The **inclusion/exclusion decision is the core intellectual work** — set criteria from your
  questions, apply them consistently, record everything, and revise honestly.
- When stuck, **read existing reviews** to see how others decided.
- **Absence of evidence and counterexamples are findings.** Reported well, they strengthen the review.
- **Operationalize, don't summarize** — show a concept's incremental value and where it applies.
- **Grey literature is allowed** — searching unpublished work (reports, theses, preprints, OpenAlex,
  arXiv) fights publication bias and strengthens the review.

There is far more nuance in the original — on meta-analysis versus narrative review versus
meta-synthesis, search strategy, study-quality tools, and the replication-crisis argument for why
reviews matter more than ever. For that, read the paper. This was the part of it I most wanted to
think out loud about first.
